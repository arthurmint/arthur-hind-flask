Inductors and Capacitors

What are passive components?

Passive components refer to components that dissipate and store energy. That is, they do not generate energy. So far, these notes have only covered one passive element, the resistor. The resistor is known as a passive linear component, since the current has a linear relationship with the voltage across it, Ohm's Law. There other forms of passive component, capacitors and inductors. These differ to resistors for several reasons:

Resistors $V = I \times R$ Dissipate energy as heat
Capacitors $I = C \times \frac {dV}{dt}$ C - capacitance Store energy in an electric field
Inductors $V = L \times \frac {dI}{dt}$ L - inductance Store energy in a magnetic field

Under DC Conditions

Whilst capacitors and inductors are linear components, they do not operate quite like resistors. The current flowing through a capcitor is proportional to the rate of change of Voltage across it. This means that, when the Voltage is fixed, no current flows. The opposite is the case for inductors, where voltage is porportional to the rate of change of current, Therefore, under perfect DC conditions, where the current does not change, the voltage across an inductor is zero.

Under DC conditions, it is easy to deal with inductors and capacitors:

  • Inductors behave like a short-circuit, under the assumption that their resistance is 0 Ω
  • Capacitors behave like an open-circuit, under the assumption that their resistance is infinite.

Under AC Conditions

Under a.c. conditions, these components act similarly to resistance and Ohm's law applies. Inductors and capacitors are known as reactive components as their effect on the circuit is determined by their reactance. Reactance does not just change the magnitude of the current for a given voltage, but it changes the phase of the current, i.e. how much current leads or lags the voltage. Reactance (units: Ohms, Ω) depends on two factors, the frequency of the source and the inductance or capacitance of the device. The formulae are $$X_C = \frac 1 {\omega C} = \frac 1 {2 \pi f C}$$ $$X_L = \omega L = 2 \pi L$$ Resistances and reactances contribute to the overall impeadance. Impeadance is usually represented using $Z$ and also has units Ohms, Ω. $$Z = R + jX$$ Here, $j$ is an imaginary number where Z is complex. X is the sum of $X_C$ and $X_L$ $$Z = R + j\omega L - j \frac 1 {\omega C}$$ Using complex representation, is is easy to apply Ohm's law: $V = I \times Z$.
