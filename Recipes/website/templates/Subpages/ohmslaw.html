Ohm's Law

What is Ohm's Law?

Ohm's law is a fundamental relationship that affects almost every electric circuit. It shows that the votlage across a load is approximately proportional to the current flowing through it.

Consider a simple DC circuit consisting of a resistor, with resistance R and a voltage source of V Volts. $$V = I \times R$$ Where $V$ is voltage (Volts), $I$ is current (Amps) and $R$ is resistance (Ohms) showing that the voltage across a resistive load is proportional to the current flowing through it.

It is important to note that the direction of the voltage opposes the flow of current through a passive device, this is why it is known as a voltage "drop". This is because passive devices consume energy. Resistors, for example, dissipate energy in the form of heat.

Example 1

For example, if the current flowing through a resistor of 1kΩ is 100mA, what is the voltage drop across the resistor?

$$V = IR$$ $$V = 100mA \times 1kΩ$$ $$V = 100 \space V$$

Resistors in series

Resistors connected together in series are simple to work with. Their individual resistances can be added together and represented with a single resistor.

$$R_T = R_1 + R_2 + R_3 +...$$

Example 2

Consider the following circuit where R1 = 100Ω, R2 = 500Ω, R3 = 1.5kΩ. We need to find the current in the circuit.



First, the total resistance can be found using this rule: $R_T = 100 + 500 + 1.5\times 10^3 = 2.1kΩ$ A new circuit can be drawn to represent this.



Using this circuit, the current can be easily found using Ohm's Law. $$I = \frac VR$$ $$I = \frac {5}{2.1 \times 10^3}$$ $$I = 2.38 mA$$

Resistors in parallel

Resistors connected in parallel are combined differently as the current is effectively shared between each resistor depending on its resistance. Their individual resistances can still be combined and represented by a single resistor using this relationship:

$$\frac 1{R_T} = \frac 1{R_1} + \frac1{R_2} + \frac 1{R_3} +...$$

Which can be calculated quickly using:

$$R_T = \frac {R_1R_2R_3\times ...}{R_1R_2 + R_1R_3 +R_2R_3 +...}$$

Example 3

Consider the following resistors in parallel with values R1 = 5kΩ, R2 = 15kΩ and R3 = 1kΩ



In order to find the voltage drop across the resistors, we can combine them into one resistor and apply Ohm's law.



$$R_T = \frac {5 \times 15 \times 1}{5 \times 15 + 15\times 1 + 5\times 1} kΩ$$ $$R_T = \frac {75}{75 + 15 + 5} kΩ$$ $$R_T = \frac {75}{95} kΩ$$ $$R_T = 0.789 k\Omega$$

Now Ohm's law can be applied

$$V= IR$$ $$V= 19mA \times 0.789kΩ$$ $$V= 15V$$ {% endblock %}
